# Co-inhabiting the dry line — reactivating the Barranco de Valcuerna
_A4 memory text, draft for Sonya to edit. Candasnos · Los Monegros · Barranco de Valcuerna. Reference species: Iberian lynx. Written arid-honest; no lush gardens._

**The premise.** Los Monegros is not empty. It is a gypsum-and-salt steppe that reads as desert to a passing eye and as a full, specialised ecosystem to anyone who stops. Our site sits at Candasnos, on the Barranco de Valcuerna, a dry ravine that once carried water and now carries mostly runoff. We take the Iberian lynx as a lens, not because it lives here today, but because what a lynx needs to move through a landscape is exactly what this landscape has lost: continuous cover, a reliable prey base, and safe ways across the things people have built. Design for the lynx and you design for the whole steppe.

**What the analysis shows.** Reading the territory from real data rather than generic land cover, three facts organise everything. First, the substrate is a badland: gypsum and mudstone erosion make up half the study area, and the Valcuerna is a gully system, not a river. Second, the living matrix is already there but fragmented: roughly 116,000 hectares of scrub, ecotone and riparian cover, threaded by 1,279 kilometres of protected drovers' roads (cañadas) that the law keeps in public hands. Third, the corridor is severed: of sixteen points where the modelled lynx path meets a barrier, fourteen are open cuts with no crossing, and the whole 93-kilometre connection hangs on two of them. The problem is not scarcity of habitat. It is discontinuity.

**The strategy.** Reactivate the Barranco de Valcuerna as an ecological spine, and stitch the fragments to it. The move is deliberately low-tech and cheap, because aridity rewards patience over spectacle. Along the ravine, loose-rock check dams and one-rock dams slow and spread the sporadic floods so water infiltrates and sediment collects; those fertile wedges become planting benches for a verified xeroriparian palette, deep-rooted and native, from the arto and retama of the slopes to the tamarisk and ash of the wetter reaches. Across the farmed matrix, the cañadas carry hedgerows and un-ploughed field corners, adding cover on public land without expropriating a single hectare. At the pinch points, a handful of vegetated underpasses and canal ramps reopen the severed corridor. In the steppe cores, artificial warrens and micro-catchments rebuild the rabbit prey base directly, which is the honest way to invite a predator that visits productive ground more than it settles.

**The loop.** These interventions are meant to compound. Erosion control traps water and sediment; vegetation establishes; cover and fruit bring back rabbits and, behind them, the predators and the birds; roaming fauna disperse seed and the steppe re-vegetates itself; a legible, wilder landscape draws trekking and mountain-bike ecotourism along the cañada network; that revenue and attention make a lower-input, regenerative agriculture viable, which returns cover to the edges and feeds back into the start. Nothing here depends on making Monegros green. It depends on making its own water and its own fauna do more work.

**The case for spending.** Every element is chosen to be defensible to a public purse. The corridor backbone is already public land under the 1995 drovers'-roads law, so the connective tissue costs planning, not purchase. The erosion structures are rock and labour. The crossings are few and targeted, unlocking a 93-kilometre corridor for the price of a handful of passages. And the outcome is not a park but a working, inhabited landscape where restoration, farming and recreation share the same ground. Co-inhabiting the dry line is the argument: people, agriculture and the lynx's world are not asked to occupy separate zones, but to reoccupy the same one, more carefully.
