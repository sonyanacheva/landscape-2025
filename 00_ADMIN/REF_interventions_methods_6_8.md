# §6b/§6c + §8c/§8d — Intervention methods & scheme content
_Drafts for the strategy/temporal panels. Erosion + flood-establishment methods verified against dryland-restoration literature (rock-detention structures). Arid-honest throughout: this is a water-harvesting steppe restoration, not a garden._

---
## §8c — Erosion mechanism (name it right)
The Monegros badlands erode by **concentrated surface runoff**, in the standard progression:
1. **Sheet (interrill) erosion** — thin, uniform removal of the surface after rain hits bare gypsum/lutite soil.
2. **Rill erosion** — runoff concentrates into small channels a few cm deep.
3. **Gully erosion** — rills deepen and coalesce into the barranco/gully network; this is the dominant, most visible process here (the Valcuerna is a gully system).

Drivers: sparse cover + crusting gypsiferous soils + intense sporadic downpours on ~350 mm/yr. The fix is not to stop water but to **harvest it**: slow and spread concentrated flow so it infiltrates, and add **deep-rooted vegetation** to bind soil and use subsurface moisture (the §8e palette is chosen for deep roots). Correct terms to use on the panel: *surface runoff · sheet–rill–gully*, NOT "flash flood" as the erosion agent.

## §8d — Flood-establishment method (corrected)
Establish vegetation by **working with** the sporadic floods, using low-tech, low-cost structures (verified: rock-detention is the recognised dryland approach):
- **Rock-detention structures** across the gullies/channels:
  - **Loose-rock check dams (gully plugs)** — small barricades that slow concentrated flow, trap sediment + water, and create fertile pockets where natives re-establish.
  - **One-rock dams (trincheras / spreaders)** — single-layer rock lines that slow *and spread* sheet/rill flow for infiltration.
  - **Gabions** (rock-filled baskets) only at larger channels.
- **Seed pelleting / seed balls** — coat native seed in clay/compost so it survives on the surface until a flow event, resisting predation and desiccation.
- **Flood-timed sowing** — sow into the autumn–winter window (see §8e) so germination coincides with the moist, cool season and the sediment traps hold water.
This replaces the earlier "cork-tap" idea. Sequence: build structures → they trap sediment + moisture → sow pelleted seed / plant nursery stock into the fertile trapped sediment → cover establishes → structures + roots further slow flow (positive loop).

Sources: Frontiers 2021 *Dryland Watershed Restoration with Rock Detention Structures*; USDA-ARS low-tech eroded-land restoration; SSWM/Akvopedia check-dams & gully-plugs; Lancaster, *Rainwater Harvesting for Drylands*.

## §6b — Feedback-loop diagram (draft)
A regenerative loop, arid-honest (each arrow is a mechanism, not a wish):
```
   Erosion control (check dams, one-rock dams on slopes + barranco)
                 │ traps sediment + water → infiltration
                 ▼
   Vegetation establishes (xeroriparian spine + scrub/ecotone)
                 │ cover + fruit (Ziziphus, Retama) + rabbit refugia
                 ▼
   Fauna return (rabbit prey base → lynx; birds, insects)
                 │ seed dispersal by roaming fauna
                 ▼
   Autonomous re-vegetation + soil building
                 │ landscape legibility + wildlife = draw
                 ▼
   Ecotourism (cañada trekking/MTB, hides, interpretation)
                 │ revenue + stewardship incentive
                 ▼
   Regenerative agriculture (financially viable, lower-input)
                 │ hedgerows, un-ploughed corners → back to cover
                 └──────────────► (reinforces Erosion control + Vegetation)
```
Keep the visual arid: ochre/gypsum palette, sparse. The point: restoration pays for itself through prey→predator recovery, ecotourism, and viable regenerative farming, all anchored on cheap water-harvesting structures.

## §6c — Forman-inspired scheme-sections (per intervention type)
Short cross-section schemes + the stakeholder argument for each (why spend):
1. **Barranco check-dam terrace** (Left Ravine): section of a gully with stepped one-rock/check dams, sediment wedges, xeroriparian planting on the wedges. *Argument:* cheapest possible erosion control; turns a degrading gully into a moisture-storing planting bench.
2. **Cañada hedgerow** (Ag Crossing): section across a drover's road with a double hedgerow + un-ploughed field-corner. *Argument:* on public land (Ley 3/1995) = no expropriation; stitches cover across the hostile matrix; pollinator + prey benefit.
3. **Fauna crossing** (barrier pinch points): section of a road/canal with a vegetated underpass / canal ramp. *Argument:* 14 of 16 corridor crossings currently have no structure; a few targeted passages unlock the whole 93 km corridor.
4. **River stepping-stone** (River Interface): section of a riparian patch on the Cinca/Ebro edge. *Argument:* small riparian nodes let the corridor reach the river network.
5. **Warren + water-harvest field** (steppe core): artificial rabbit warrens + micro-catchments. *Argument:* builds the prey base directly (the "visited, not lived-in" logic) at trivial cost.

Each scheme: existing condition (degraded) vs proposed (restored), with the low cost and the public-land / no-expropriation angle foregrounded for the funding case.
